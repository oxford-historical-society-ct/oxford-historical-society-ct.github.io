1883_07-20_Seymour-Record

 

OXFORD.

 

Mrs. F. O. Cable, son and daughter, of Owego, N. Y. are making their summer visit here.

 

E. N. Tucker has retired from his doughnut business and has gone to Bowman's, New Haven, to look into the art of photography.

 

Mr. McCracken accompanied Rev. Mr. Roberts to Ansonia last Sunday afternoon to assist at the evening service in Christ's Church.

 

H. C. Osborn having all the business that he can attend to in Seymour has discontinued his meat route through Oxford, for the present at least.

 

Mrs. H. B. Perry of New Haven, who is spending a few weeks at the home of her youth, the John Beecher homestead, has been ander the doctor's care for a week.

 

Rev. Mr. Pardee of Seymour is expected to preach in St. Peter's church next Sunday afternoon, service to commence at 2 o'clock. The morning service and Sunday school will be omitted.

 

The Oxford Advertiser for July 18th says that "Royal Chatfield got poisened." How's that, Royal? Is your life insured and some one going for the premium, or have you been interviewing the festive ivy and the frisky sumach ?

 

When Dr. John Lounsbury kept a flour and feed store at the foot of the Sandbank hill in Seymour years ago, his his rhyming ability was well known. When he went to his meals he left this notice on the door :

"The best of flour from Genesee, Made from the choicest wheat;

Deacon Kinney keeps the key, And lives across the street."

 

SOUTHFORD.

 

The Rev. H. Schofleld will preach in the chapel next Sunday evening at 7 o'clock; afternoon service at 2 and Sunday school at 3 as usual.

 

It is hoped that no one will infer from the notice in last week's Record that "Jumbo Jum" will perform out in the streets next Wednesday and Thursday evenings. As it would not be expedient to let him loose he will be exhibited in Southford Chapel after the drama "Out in the Streets" has been presented by a cast of ten characters. Music will be interspersed between the acts and refreshments served after the entertainment. Admission 25 cents. Children, 15 cents.

 

 

GENERAL TOM THUMB.

 

General Tom Thumb, whose real name is Charles Heywood Stratton, died at his residence, in Middleboro, Mass.. Sunday morning, of apoplexy. The deceased was born in Bridgeport, Jan. 4, 1838. In 1852 he entered the services of P. T. Barnum.

 

At that time he was not two feet high and weighed less than sixteen pounds. He was, however, a well formed, bright little fellow, with light hair and rosy cheeks, and the great showman engaged him at a salary of $3 a week and travelling expenses. Before he had been a year with Barnum his salary was increased to 825 a week.

 

In 1854 Barnum took the diminutive prodigy to England, and had a most successful season with him at the Princess' Theatre London. He was afterward presented to Queen Victoria.

 

After this Barnum took him to Paris, and presented him to King Louis Phillippe and King Leopold, of Belgium, and reaped quite a golden harvest. In 1862 a dwarf girl named Lavinia Warren, of considerable personal attractions, was engaged by Mr. Barnum.

 

After some time she became the wife of the General, Feb. 14, 1863. The happy pair were popular favorites and made much money. But the General was rather extravagant and spent all he earned. At one time he owned a fine yacht and indulged in other aristocratic and fashionable luxuries. Of late yenrs he was not so well off, and was under the necessity of showing in small towns which in the heighth of his prosperity he would have despised. His wife survives him.
