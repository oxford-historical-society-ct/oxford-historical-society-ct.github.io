1882_03-02_Seymour-Record

 

GREAT HILL.

 

The pastor of the Methodist Church at Great Hill has been encouraged by a good congregation generally, especially during the present year; but last Sunday he found the church as warm as summer without any one there to enjoy it. The impression appeared to be, as the pastor was somewhat indisposed, he would not venture out in such foggy weather. If not stormy from noon to 1 o'clock, in case the pastor is not able to preach, some of the active and zealous brethren will go from Seymour to conduct a service there.

 

SQUANTUCK.

 

Henry Wheeler is expecting to take place lately vacated by Mr. Hoyt.

 

A. J. Downs has lost several turkeys and some honey by theives this winter.

 

The ice remains firm on the river yet. It only rose about six inches during the rain of last Sunday.

 

E. D. Tomlinson is getting out several thousand feet of flooring for the Birmingham Iron Foundry.

 

James Hoyt has moved out of Peter Tomlinson's house and gone to New Milford with his family.

 

Henry Rose has erected temporary residence in the woods where he is chopping and is keeping a bachelor's hall.

 

Frank E. Smith and wife have taken up their residence with Edwin Tomlinson for the coming year. Mrs. Smith will take charge of the housekeeping.

 

 

QUAKER FARMS.

 

Mrs. Jeanette Hine is quite sick with pneumonia.

 

The measles are  quite fashionable and cheap around here; in fact they give them away.

 

The church is open for services every Wednesday evening through Lent. Rev. Mr. Bailey officiating.

 

Several parties around here have sold their tobacco, some to Staub & Bristol, of New Milford, and some to Chas. Hall of Sandy Hook, but they do not feel very crank about the prices received.

 

Mr. H. E. Tomlinson is improving his residence with a new porch in front. But it's no use, neighbor T., if you want to be in style you should get the measles.

 

The old familiar mill whistle  now resound again through the valley at regular intervals, with, one long whistle at 5, two whistles at 61/2, and three at 7 a.m., one as 12 M, and one at 1 and 6 p.m.

 

The neighbors and friends of Mr. C. C. Rider gave him quite a surprise on Thursday evening (22d), and presented him with a fine copy of Webster's Unabridged Dictionary, as a token of their appreciation of his services as Lay Reader and Sunday School Superintendent. Mr. Geo. Skidmore made the presentation with some very appropriate remarks, which were responded to by the recipient with thanks. Unfortunately the evening was somewhat stormy so that but a small portion of those who intended to be present were on hand.

 

 

OXFORD.

 

Several report effects of the earthquake shock Tuesday night.

 

The ladies of St. Peter's Church are engaged in home missionary work.

 

Nelson P. Williams, who has been in failing health for some time, is considable poorer.

 

H. E. Bidwell, of Quaker Farms, has again gone to Rome, N. Y., to be treated by a cancer doctor.

 

Rev. Mr. Bailey did not go to the Farms last Sunday morning on account of the storm, consequently will go next Sunday afternoon. There will therefore be service in St. Peter's Church at 7 p.m. in addition to the morning service.

 

Wm. M. Hubbell having bargained of F. W. Hubbell for the place at the corner of the turnpike and Christian street, will move there this Spring.

 

G. W. Cable and his sister, Mrs. M. A. Doolittle of Seymour, started the first of the week on a visit to their brother, H. R. Cable, at Goshen, Orange Co., N. Y.

 

Charles M., son of Wm. M. Hubbell; has gone into the employ of the Frost Manufacturing Company, at Marion, Hartford Co.

 

Mrs. Emma Northrop and her daughter, widow of the late H. A. Lum, expect soon to occupy the Deacon Joel White place, in the Center, now occupied by Frank A. Tucker.

 

If Jno. B. Pope moves out of town this spring, as he now contemplates, this, with the removal of L. B. Perkins, will leave Geo. R. Baldwin the sole selectman until the two vacancies are filled.

 

Dr. Barnes is very busy just now, and his patients, several of whom are afflicted with the measles, are scattered about as follows: Red Oak, Riggs street, Bower's Hill, Five Mile Hill, Ell Lane, etc.
