1882_01-26_SEYMOUR RECORD.

 

OXFORD

 

James H. Bartlett, who satisfactorily represented this town in the House in 1879, has again been called to perform that office. He is now fifty-two years of age, having been born in Washington, Dutchess County, New York, on October 7, 1829. He was educated in the common schools, and about the year 185 removed to Freeport, Ill., where he engaged in mercantile business. He remained there a number of years, and was one of its aldermen in 1858 and 1859. He is at present, and has been for nine years, a selectman of Oxford, and has abandoned mercantile pursuits for farming. He acts with the Republican Party. In 1852 he voted with Gen. Scott, in New York City, and did active political work in the Freemont campaign of 1856, and was also an ardent supporter of Abraham Lincoln.

 

Rev. Mr. Snow holds service at Bowers Hill next Sunday at 3:30 p.m.

 

The Congregational social will be held in the vestibule next Wednesday afternoon and evening.

 

Miss Foot having closed the winter term in the Riggs street district is teaching an eight weeks' term in the Christain street district. Miss F. has also been engaged to teach the spring and summer term at Riggs street.

 

There will be an oyster supper and dance at the Oxford House hall on Tuesday evening, Jan. 31st, to which all are invited. Tickets for dance, 50c. Supper, 50c per couple.

 

SOUTHFORD

 

Miss Jessie Curtiss of this place, who has been teaching on Hull's Hill, closed her school last Saturday.

 

There was another large party held in Henry S. Wheeler's building near the depot, last Friday evening, music was furnished by Bliss' band of Monroe, as usual.

 

C. A. Bunnell has completed his arrangements for business and solicits a share of the public patronage in the way of horse and ox shoeing and general jobbing.

 

A young man called at the house of Mrs. Jane Munson on Hull's Hill one night last week and wanted to stay all night. She very kindly took him in. The next morning, while her son was out doing chores, he saw the fellow running down across the lots. He went to the house and found a coin receiver that contained a $5 gold piece and some other change was missing. He immediately hitched up his horses, took in Daniel P. Johnson and started in pursuit. They found him at the house of F. H Gray on George's Hill, where they recovered the receiver and money and the fellow promised he would clear out, so they let him go.
