A MEMORIAL SERMON PREACHED UPON THE OCCASION OF THE DEATH OF NATHAN J. WILCOXSON, ESQ., IN THE CONGREGATIONAL CHURCH IN OXFORD, OCT 8TH, 1882 BY REV. F. E. SNOW.

 

Text: The just man walketh in his integrity. Proverbs 20:7

 

In these days the funeral sermon is very largely a thing of the past; and this is so not because the church has less love and respect for her honored members nor because the pulpit holds in any lower esteem the services of laymen; but in the spread of free education and the greater participation of a proportion of every community in public affairs, individuality has become to a greater extent swallowed up. The multiplication of newspapers with their marvelous facilities for gathering the news of the whole civilized world, contributes to the same end. Men know more of the outside world and care more for the contests of nations and the prosperity of communities than for the struggles of individual hearts and the success or troubles of individual lives.

 

A passing tribute beside the open coffin of respect to the dead or of praise of his virtues and excellences, very largely, takes the place of the funeral sermon both in the demands of the people and the disposition of the preacher. This church has been called upon within the short period of two months, to part with two of its most efficient members and valued officers, Deacon Reuben B. Curtis and Bro. Nathan J. Wilcoxson. It is fitting that in a memorial service like this, the names of these two, associated for so many years in love and service to this church, should be mentioned together. The prosperity of this church was dear to them both, and to that end they gave much of the best years of their lives. We give equal honor to both in our memory of their labors. Their ; names will be cherished by their townsmen in loving remembrance until our names like theirs, shall have passed from the living, and shall have no other place than upon the marble and granite that mark our last resting places or upon the musty pages of the church records. If the life and labors of the one receives more notice than those of the other from this pulpit, it is only because he was to a greater extent identified with the interests of this community.

 

Nathan J. Wilcoxson was the son of David and Betsey Johnson Wilcoxson, and was born in the town of Brookfield, Conn., May 12, 1796. He was the oldest of a family of six children, of whom but one, a sister, is now living. Of the early days of his childhood and youth very little is known. As was almost universally the case in those days, he was bound out when quite a lad to learn a trade, but this trade being distasteful to him, he abandoned it as soon as he attained his majority. His mother was a woman of true piety, as was also the aunt under whose care he came when he left home. To these two Godly women is to be attributed, without doubt, much of the formation of his character.

 

Early in life he gave evidence of a serious and studious cast of mind. He snatched from his hours of toil every possible moment for study. In company with a fellow apprentice he devoted his evenings to this. He had few educational advantages, but by private study he had fitted himself to teach, and upon attaining to his majority he sought further opportunities to cultivate the bent of his mind in this direction.

 

He taught one winter in Norwich. Conn., to which place his father removed when Nathan was about five years old. He afterwards taught in various places, still devoting his spare hours to study. He spent two years in Hempstead, L. I., as assistant in an academy, and upon closing his school there was engaged to teach in an orphan asylum in New York.

 

Here, owing to his too close application to his studies his health broke down. He returned to Newtown, where, upon the death of his father, which occurred about this time, he established, by his own unaided efforts, his mother and sister in a house of their own.

 

He came to Oxford, Oct. 30th, 1825, when he was in the 30th year of his age. It was not his purpose to remain here permanently, but circumstances so shaped his life that he very soon took his residence in this place. He taught one year in the school building still standing a few rods to the east of this spot. He was married in June, 1827, to Anna Blackman of Huntington, who died Oct. 3d, 1870, at the age of 73 years. His second marriage occurred May 23d, 1871.

 

At the close of his year's engagement at the district school he organized a private school which he taught two years and a half. He was then appointed Deputy Sheriff for New Haven County, an office which he held for ten years. From the close of this period he taught more or less for thirty years, until 1870, when he took the district school for six weeks to teaching in the same building where he had begun his educational work forty-five years before.

 

He was postmaster fourteen years in succession, through administrations of both political parties, a fact worth recording in these days of frequent political changes. He represented the town in the state legislature in 1842 through two sessions. At that time it was the custom to choose the clerks of the house from the roll of its members. Mr. Wilcoxson was chosen the second clerk, and compiled and printed the records of the house, being the first clerk to do this. He was town clerk for 20 years and school visitor for more than 50 years. He was judge of the probate court for 16 years. At the close of that period he resigned the office on account of age, but was immediately appointed clerk of the court, a position which he held to the day of his death.

 

There is no record available for use at this time to show when Mr. Wilcoxson made a public profession of religion. It was probably in early manhood in Newtown. It appears that he must have connected himself with this church immediately upon taking his residence in Oxford. He has served the church and society in many capacities. He became a member of the society upon his own petition in 1827, and in the year following was elected society's clerk, which position he occupied until 1838. He served the society as its treasurer for several years and in 1872 was again appointed its clerk, being still in that office at his death.

 

Upon the resignation of Deacon Curtis as clerk of the church in 1874, Mr. Wilcoxson was appointed in his place, and he was serving the church in that capacity up to their last public meeting before his death. He was constantly giving of his time and labor to promote the well-being of this church and society.

 

Such is a very meagre sketch of the life of Mr. Wilcoxson. The materials for making it have been found to be very imperfect and these facts have been compiled and arranged while under the shadow of a heavy personal affliction, and the consequent distractions of mind has added to the meagerness and insufficiency of the record.

 

But from even this brief sketch we see Mr. Wilcoxson in a threefold light, in his private character, in his capacity as a public man and that of church officer. A few thoughts under each of these heads will not be inappropriate.

 

So little is known of the early years of his life that it is impossible to say when Christian experience began, but it must have been when a child. It appears that at a very early age he began to feel the responsibility of life and its graver side was even then as always, before his mind. This fact gave color to his whole life. If his character could be epitomized in one word it would be that fine old word, Puritan. He was in his youth thoughtful and serious. He never indulged in frivolity or even in the common gayeties of youth and it is the testimony of the only remaining member of his family that his manner was as young in the last days of his life as in his early youth and manhood. He was dignified and correct in conversation. He hated reckless frivolity and waste of time.

 

As a student he was laborious and methodical. He studied with his finger upon the page and following each word and when he had gone over a page of any text book in this careful manner it was his, he never had to commit it a second time. When he began the study of arithmetic he carefully copied every example, even in simple addition, into blank books which he always preserved and which have been shown me since his death. The paper is yellow with time, but the penmanship is even and regular and as clear and distinct, as though written yesterday. Thus he studied through arithmetic and algebra and geometry.

 

It was his habit all through his life to read as he studied. He would in the last year of his life read an article m the newspaper, and after one reading would be able to give every fact and almost the exact language of what he had read.

 

He believed that an education was the best thing to be won from this life, and he set himself about acquiring it in a systematic way. The methodical habits thus formed remained with him all his life and were apparent in everything he did. So confirmed did he become in these habits that he could not make a motion or draw up a warning for a meeting except in conformity to them. And if we have at times chafed while under their influence, we should remember that to such systematic habits was due much of Mr. Wilcoxson's success in life.

 

He was a man of iron will. He constantly endeavored to know the right, and having discovered it as he thought, he was inflexible. Even though he might be in the wrong, if he thought himself right, nothing could move him. Yet he ever held himself open to conviction, but the evidence must be very clear and positive. He could bend but only to a superior power.

 

He was a man of wonderful retentiveness of memory. It would seem as though no fact nor name nor date which once gained lodgment in his mind was ever dislodged therefrom. His mind was a storehouse of facts in regard to the history, not only of his own town, but of the county and state.

 

He was a self-made, self-educated man, if it be proper to speak thus without acknowledging God's guidance and help. He never inherited any property, but acquired what he possessed by his own efforts, unaided by any outside assistance. The only presents of any value that he ever received were a load of wood and a silver-headed cane.

 

Above all he was a Christian man. If it should be thought by some that in this particular he seemed too methodical and unemotional, it was not because he was unfeeling.

 

His type of religion was the natural manifestation of his traits of character. He never could indulge in the ecstasies so common in some phases of religion, nor even in ordinary enthusiasm. It was inconsistent with his nature so to do. If religion in him took on a sober cast, it was not somber or gloomy. He was ever hopeful, of his usefulness as a Christian, of the prosperity of this church, of the prolongation of his life even down to the day of his death, of a blessed immortality when life should end.

 

The service of God was to him a very dignified and serious service and his patience was sorely tried by anyone who engaged in that service with flippancy or carelessness of manner. His natural characteristics were apparent in him as a man of prayer. The old phrases which had been familiar to his lips for so many years were dear to him, and it was hard for him to drop them. They meant more to him than to others.

 

He was very systematic in regard to family prayers. Worship in the family was as much a part of his daily life as the breakfast which preceded it. On the last day of his life, when the morning meal was over his Bible was placed on the table, he read a chapter from one of the gospels and led in prayer.

 

It has been apparent to those who have ministered to him in these last few months that his Christian character was ripening for heaven. Much of the sternness was gone from his manner, and his prayers and the general tone of his conversation have given evidence of a mellowness and gentleness that could come alone from nearness to the other world. He desired to live, but he said repeatedly that when his time came he was fully ready to go.

 

Turning from this view of the man let us think of him in his public capacity. He was first of all an educator, not, it is true, in any very extensive way as many would look at it. But I think if the effect of his training upon the minds of all the pupils who came under his care could be summed up, the grand result would be a tribute worthy of any man.

 

Many of you know his methods and the nature of his work better than I do. He endeavored to make his scholars as methodical and thorough as himself. To expect this of any large proportion of a class was perhaps too much to ask of boy nature. In one sense of the word be was never a boy himself and consequently he had too little sympathy with boyish propensity. Yet he was kindly disposed to all young people and nothing gratified him more than evidences of ability and future usefulness in the young. He rejoiced in their successes. He would do anything to contribute to their success.

 

If his methods were a little old-fashioned they have been the basis of the success in after life of more than one of his many pupils. He loved to give a word of encouragement to struggling youth, and he was intensely gratified at any appreciation of such encouragement. Some years ago while acting as school visitor, he called on the school at Five Mile Hill. Among the children he noticed a colored boy who, by his bright answers showed good ability. Mr. Wilcoxson sat down by his side and questioned him further, and then said to him "if you study and are diligent you have the ability to make a man of mark."

 

That boy afterwards became the Hon. E. B. Bassett, U. S. Minister to Hayti. Mr. Bassett ascribes all his success in life to the encouragement which he received from Mr. Wilcoxson, and he has been in the habit of repeatedly sending to Mr. Wilcoxson, pamphlets and papers in recognition of the latter's kindness and encouragement.

 

The venerable Rev. Andrew L. Stone, D.D., a man known to all the churches and held in honor by them all, took his first Latin lessons under Mr. Wilcoxson's instruction.  The last time Dr. Stone was in Oxford he took pains to make personal acknowledgment to his old teacher of the good impulses which he had received from the latter's teaching.

 

These two incidents may serve to show something of the far reaching influence of Mr. Wilcoxson's life as a teacher. We must not linger longer at this point, only to say that all his work as an instructor was pervaded by the individuality of character which we have seen he possessed.

 

Mr. Wilcoxson was a politician. He united his fortunes to the Democratic Party and remained all his life-long a consistent democrat. He enjoyed many honors at the hands of his party. At a very early period in the existence of the New Haven Register, if not at the beginning of its publication, he became one of its subscribers, and it remained his political paper to his death. For many years he kept a perfect file of this paper and it is an illustration, not only of his wonderful powers of memory, but also of his methodical order that he could find at a moment's notice any item of political news that he wished. It is a singular fact that his last subscription to this paper expired the day before his death.

 

Though he was a consistent partisan he was not a bitter one, and could regard with charity and respect, one who differed in political views. I remember to have heard him speak with great admiration of the late President Garfield.

 

I think it may be said without equivocation that as a public man he walked in his integrity. When he was running for state senator from this district it is related of him that he was approached by some of the party men with the assurance that if he would make a liberal contribution they would secure his election. He refused this offer with the indignant declaration that he had never given one dollar to purchase a vote and he never should.  He was defeated, that too in a democratic district, but he had the reflection with which to console himself that it was for conscience' sake. It was his purpose to be upright and honorable. If he ever failed it was not for Lack of right motives and a determined purpose.

 

Among his public services ought to be mentioned the following items. He was chiefly instrumental in securing the mail route through this town. Before the establishment of the probate court in this district all business of that nature was transacted in New Haven. Mr. Wilcoxson, appreciating the difficulties and inconvenience consequent upon such an arrangement, took measures for securing a probate court for the town of Oxford, in which he was finally successful. To him then we are indebted for our mail facilities and for exemption from much of the inconvenience which attaches to the transaction of probate business.

 

But I must hasten on to speak of Mr. Wilcoxson in his relation to this church and society. I have found the records very unfruitful on this point. He must have united with this church at that stormy period of its history when it was torn by dissensions, the only method of healing which seemed the obliteration of the records. There is quite a gap in them therefore, but even this heroic treatment did not remedy the evil. The records which follow furnish evidence of troublous times and are very imperfect. This is near the date of his coming to Oxford.

 

A few years afterwards he became interested in Sabbath school work from having attended a conference in another place, and organized a Sabbath school in connection with this church. He was its first superintendent and continued in that office for many years. He was identified with its history until 1872, when he retired from any active participation in it.

 

I have already given the record of his joining himself to the society and his almost immediate election to the position as clerk. If I should choose one word to characterize his relation to the church and society it would be faithfulness. He is a standing rebuke to us, my brethren, for he was always in his place. No personal inconvenience kept him away from the Sabbath service. He never missed a communion service until the last one.

 

I never shall forget what was to me a very pathetic sight when, near the close of the sermon on the last communion Sabbath but one, yonder door opened and the venerable man walked slowly down the aisle to his accustomed seat. I felt like stopping and waiting in respectful silence till he was seated. The infirmity of age and the hand of disease sat heavily upon him, but he felt that he must be in his place. Many of us felt then that we should never see him in this place again, and it was indeed the last time that his familiar figure was seen in the house of God, until the hands of his brethren in that fraternity which he loved, bore him to take his place yet once more among us ere he was carried to his long sleep upon the hill.

 

He was no less faithful in the conference meeting. Always there unless absolutely prevented, his voice has often admonished and instructed us or led our thoughts to the throne of grace. Brethren, were we all as faithful to our covenant vows according to the gifts that are in us as our brother was, this church would not be lacking in progress.

 

Into his work as a church and society officer was carried all his methodical and systematic ways. The very penmanship shows the man's character. Every record is entered with neatness and precision. There is no ambiguity, but the record shows at first reading just what is meant to be preserved. We shall miss his work and I think there will be conspicuously absent from the future business of this church and society much of the order and precision which his influence has hitherto given to it.

 

But he has gone from our midst. It is appropriate that in closing this fragmentary address a few words should be said of his last acts. He clung to life almost with pertinacity. His mind was bright even to the last day of his advanced age. His final act as a church officer was that of recording the baptism of five children in May last. His last act as a public officer was attendance at the meeting of the board of education only a few weeks before his death. The last time he rode out was to call upon the brother who lay sick and who lingered behind him but three days ere he too passed "into the silent land." He took his pen in hand for the last time early in September, to inscribe his autograph in a book soon to be forwarded to the widow of the honored pastor of this church for so many years, Mrs. Stephen Topliff.

 

Upon the last day of September he read his weekly paper, partook of the evening meal, and then without warning, without pain, he fell asleep. "I want nothing more" were the last words that passed his lips. What more fitting and beautiful words could close a life of such usefulness.

 

Brethren, I confess to you that I stand in the presence of the memory of this man with humility and reverence. He was a just man and walked in his integrity. He was not perfect. His best friends would be the most ready to say that. He had his peculiarities and his faults as we all do. But his failings were those of a true and noble nature. There is so much that is true and noble that we may forget the rest and let the mantle of charity forever hide every fault. And when we come to the end of life may it be with no more fear, with no more regret, than his, and with the same trust, the same courage and confidence that marked his closing days.

 

 

 

THE PROBATE COURT for the District of Oxford

 

The Application of Sarah A. Johnson respectfully showeth : That she is Guardian to Allen W Johnson, a minor belonging to said District, and that said minor is the owner of the following real estate, viz.: One undivided one half of a certain tract of land with the buildings thereon, situated in the Town of Oxford in said District and bounded as follows, to wit: Northerly by land now or lately of the heirs of George Washburn, deceased, easterly by land of Lewis Miller, southerly by land of James Swan and land of Noah Osborn and westerly by Little River; also the lane or roadway leading across said Little River from said land above described to the Oxford turnpike so-called. The same being subject to a right of flowage as appears on record in said Oxford.

 

And She Further Says, That the avails thereof are wanted for the support, maintenance, and education of said minor, and that it will be for the advantage of said minor to sell the same, and invest the avails thereof according to law; wherefore she says that there is just and reasonable cause for selling the same. She, therefore, prays the court to order said real estate to be sold, and to empower her or some other meet person to sell the same pursuant to the statute in such case made and provided. SARAH A. JOHNSON, Guardian.

Dated, September 19th, 1882.

 

DISTRICT OF OXFORD PROBATE COURT ss. Oxford, Sept. 21st, 1882. OrderedThat the foregoing application be heard and determined at the Probate Office in Oxford, on the 16th day of October, 1882, at one o'clock, afternoon; and that notice thereof be published in some public newspaper near the place where such real estate lies, three weeks successively, before said time assigned.

 

5i6-3t                  LEWIS BARNES, Judge.

 

 

 

OXFORD.

 

Frances J. McEwen, aged 76, widow of the late David J. McEwen, died suddenly on Friday morning about 3 o'clock. The deceased had not been feeling as well as usual for a few days.
